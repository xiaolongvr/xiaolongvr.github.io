A Short Bio

I am an Assistant Professor of Computer Science at UC Santa Barbara, co-director of the MIRAGE Lab, and affiliated faculty in the Four Eyes Lab. Before joining UCSB, I received my Ph.D. degree from the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Sciences at UC Berkeley, advised remotely by Prof. Ravi Ramamoorthi at UC San Diego. During my Ph.D., I worked at Walt Disney Animation Studios (2014), Autodesk (2015), Weta Digital (2016) and NVIDIA Research (2017) as an intern. Earlier, I obtained my bachelor degree in Computer Science from Tsinghua University in China in 2013, advised by Prof. Shi-Min Hu and Prof. Kun Xu.

My research is in Computer Graphics. During my Ph.D. career, I mainly aimed at rendering photo-realistic visual appearance (a.k.a. hard-core graphics) at real world complexity, building theoretical foundations mathematically and physically to reveal the principles of the visual world. I have brought original research topics to Computer Graphics, such as detailed rendering from microstructure, and real-time ray tracing with reconstruction. And I took the first steps exploiting Machine Learning approaches for physically based rendering.

As a young Computer Graphics researcher, my dream is to present people an interactive computer-generated world to live in, just like the ones in the movies The Matrix and Ready Player One. To achieve that, I come up with my own Rendering Equation, as you can see above. I am actively and prudently looking for outstanding graduate students (details and FAQs) to work on related research topics, including but not limited to physically-based / image-based rendering, real-time ray tracing and realistic appearance modeling / acquisition. Let's work together, not to change the world, but to create one.

Recruitment and Collaboration


First of all, if you're looking at this page, it indicates that you're interested in working with me. I would like to thank you very much for this.

In general, I plan to send out zero or one Ph.D. offer and several M.S. offers each year in my early career. For prospective master students, you are welcome to email me and discuss, but only after you are admitted to our university.

I sincerely hope that you apply only because you like Rendering, or in general, Computer Graphics, and you appreciate what I've been working on. I know you have a lot of questions. Let me organize them as Q-s (to me) and A-s (from me) below. Some of my answers can be REALLY HARSH, and I can foresee that some of you may turn away right after reading them. No problem, because I do want these words to act a pass of pre-screening. However, these are real thoughts and advices from me and I hope they can help you in your research career as well.

Q: What's your research interest?
A: First of all, DO NOT trust anyone's research interest. It can be anything unfounded. For example, I am interested in piloting a helicopter, and I'm not lying, so I can write it on my resume without feeling ashamed. But it doesn't in any sense indicate that I can actually fly a helicopter. When you look for someone's research, just look for what has been done and judge by yourself what she/he is really interested in and is capable of.
To me, my research is Rendering in Computer Graphics. And it always will be. However, I am not interested in Computer Vision and will not recruit any Computer Vision students, though I might be involved in my collaborators' research projects. While I honor the area of Computer Vision as much as you do, I must say that it is completely different from Computer Graphics. Please don't mix them. Also, I consider Machine Learning as a general tool. I would very much like to explore the possibility to apply it in Graphics, but wouldn't rely solely on it.

Q: What are the your requirements for me to work with you?
A: It is a must to have taken a Computer Graphics course. Strong knowledge in Math (esp. Calculus and Linear Algebra), Physics (esp. Optics), Programming (esp. C++ and OI/ICPC awards), Statistics. Participation in Computer Graphics research/engineering is strongly recommended.
I would like to further highlight the GRAPHICS-RELATED experience. For example, if you have written a renderer with correct bidirectional path tracing on your own, or if you have implemented several algorithms from SIGGRAPH papers in PBRT, or if you have (pre)published a paper in Pacific Graphics, you are awesome. As you can probably see already, I need proof that you are interested in Computer Graphics and you can do it well. That's it.
Experience in Python, GANs as well as general neural networks, and publications in Machine Learning and Computer Vision, will be neither useful nor harmful to me, and will simply be ignored. Just make sure there is still something left when you cross them off your resume.
I don't care much about scores. But don't stand out in an opposite way.

Q: I don't have any Graphics background but I'm a quick learner / I have a lot of CVPR papers, can I be your Ph.D. student?
A: No.

Q: I haven't heard of you previously, but I have found that your research is interesting, can I be your Ph.D. student?
A: No. If it is the first time that you hear of me, I am pretty sure that you haven't done anything related to rendering research. So it is unlikely that I will recruit you as my Ph.D. student.

Q: I hear that Assistant Professors (APs) are often "pushy", how about you?
A: No one gets a Ph.D. degree in Computer Graphics without working hard. So, when you ask this question, I cannot help wondering if you are actually ready for your Ph.D. career, and if you are really going to enjoy it. And this is serious. Please think about why you want this degree and what you are willing to pay to earn it.

Q: I am an undergrad/master/Ph.D. student and I have a great idea, may I collaborate with you on that?
A: Yes, but not personally. Let your advisor (must be a rendering researcher) contact me.

Q: Do you recruit summer interns in 2022?
A: In general, I prefer not. I need to take a complete break during summer 2022. Therefore, personal solicitations will not be considered. Please let your advisor contact me directly.
Also, my internship will only be about rendering research. It is difficult and it is quite unlikely that you'll get a paper published if you do summer internship with me. There are easier subareas in graphics, as well as much easier topics outside. So, think twice before you contact me.

Q: Do you have any positions for visiting students/scholars in the 2021-2022 academic year?
A: I may consider. If you are a student, I need informal recommendations from your advisor. When you are sending me emails, please remember to cc your recommender. This is strictly enforced, or I will not reply to your email. Visiting scholars are welcome to contact me directly, but again, (core) Computer Graphics only.

Q: Will you recruit postdocs?
A: Yes. Yet still, do not contact me personally. Let your advisor reach me.

Q: Can you give some examples of emails improperly contacting you?
A: "Of course I can imagine that you want to ask me if I am qualified in the first place. But everything has a start and no one was born experienced. If everyone recruits only experienced people, no one would."
"I’m in search of a PhD in computer science program for Fall 2021. Browsing your homepage https://www.cs.ucsb.edu/people/faculty/yan-0, I found your research area is fairly appealing to me and matches with my interest."

When Do I Consider Submitting Technical Reports


I always believe that technical reports (especially those non-anonymous) are harmful to the double blind reviewing process. The technical reports were not commonly seen in core rendering, but recently, even decent rendering researchers, both in the academia and in the industry, began to submit technical reports. Moreover, top conferences (SIGGRAPH, SIGGRAPH Asia, etc.) explicitly express that no penalties will be given based on existing Arxiv papers. Therefore, I really feel the pain deciding which side to stand on. As a result, I made the following rules for myself to determine whether to submit technical reports.

I would only consider submitting technical reports if at least one of the following conditions apply:

  1. I am not the corresponding author. Therefore, I don't decide.
  2. A coauthor of mine strongly suggests doing so, or has to do so based on e.g. company policies.
  3. I am submitting a non-rendering paper.
  4. I consider that my paper has been treated unfairly in a previous submission.

The last bullet is especially important. It happens quite often that a non-rendering expert (e.g. a random person from CV or VR) would review and judge my submission with strong (and of course, factually wrong) opinion and (out of nowhere) confidence. Once I determine that my submission was rejected because of this, I would consider immediately submitting a technical report.
